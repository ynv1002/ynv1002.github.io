Introduction¶

Introduction New Orleans is a vibrant tourist destination celebrated for its music, food, and festivals, drawing millions of visitors each year. In response to the influx of tourists, short-term rentals (STRs) have proliferated, with both local and corporate investors capitalizing on the growing demand for temporary lodging.

Since 2017, the city has sought to regulate STRs, citing concerns over housing affordability, corporate takeovers of residential properties, and neighborhood disruption (City Planning Commission, 2018, see "Affordable Housing" on p. 9).

In 2023, a new ordinance further restricted STRs, limiting permits to one per residential block and requiring property owners to reside on-site (WWNO, 2025).

While New Orleans does not explicitly name crime as a reason for regulating STRs, research from Northeastern University found a correlation between higher STR density and increased crime in Boston (PMC, 2021). The study suggests that STRs may contribute to crime by attracting transient visitors—who may be more vulnerable to or responsible for criminal activity—and by weakening community cohesion, as full-time residents are more invested in neighborhood stability than short-term guests.

Our project investigates the relationship between STR density and crime in New Orleans. Specifically, we examine whether areas with higher STR concentrations report more criminal incidents and whether crime levels influence the approval of STR applications. Using this data, we aim to develop a predictive model for incident report frequency based on STR permit counts in a given area.

Research Questions:¶

  1. Do high-density STR areas experience more crime?

  2. Are STR applications following current laws?

In this project, we investigate:

Whether active STR licenses comply with updated law restrictions Whether areas with more STR properties experience more crime Whether STR activity can help predict crime density Our datasets combine Short-Term Rental (STR) license applications and police-reported crimes in New Orleans from 2017 onward.

Observations from Exploratory Analysis of STR Data¶

We can see that over since the city began regulating STRs in 2017, there have been drastic changes to the types of applications submitted. Let's take a closer look:

  • On April 1, 2017, all Short Term Rental properties, residential or Commercial, were required to apply for a "Short Term Rental" License (https://nola.gov/nola/media/311/STR-Handbook-version-20191203.pdf)

  • On December 1, 2019, legislation drew a permit distinction between "STR Residential Owner" and "STR Commercial Owner" licenses. The new ordinance required that the property must be "Owner Occupied" and that there only be 1 STR per block face (https://www.nola.gov/nola/media/City-Planning/Final-2019-STR-Study-with-Maps.pdf)

  • On July 1, 2023, all residential STR licenses are phased out and replaced with "Non-Commercial STR" licences. The "one licence per block" rule is more strictly enforced; in the case of multiple applicants on the same block, a license is distributed by lottery. No new Commercial STR permits are accepted after June 2023 (https://nola.gov/next/short-term-rental-administration/announcements/changes-to-str-laws/)

As the colors change in the bar graph above, it represents the laws changing in the city surrounding the permiting of STRs in 2017, 2019 and 2023. We can see how passing legislation has reduced new license applications. Moving forward, we can infer that current operating STR properties are represented in the data by the following permits: the most recent Non-Commercial Short Term Rental licences (that are issued and not expired) and the Short Term Rental Commercial Owner licenses (that have not yet expired or been revoked).

We will filter for those active properties in the next section

From our initial visual exploration of the police incident data, we identified the following key insights:

  • Most Common Crime Types:
    The top reported incidents include Complaint (Other), Area Check, and Disturbance (Other). Many of these reflect general or ambiguous categories, which may not indicate serious criminal activity but still contribute significantly to the volume of police calls. This suggests a large portion of incidents involve quality-of-life concerns or precautionary checks.

  • Crime by District:
    District 3 has the highest number of reported incidents overall. However, this is likely influenced by its larger geographic area, which naturally encompasses more neighborhoods and streets.
    District 1, which includes the French Quarter, shows slightly fewer total incidents but is known for being a hotspot due to its dense nightlife and tourism—meaning incident density per square mile may actually be much higher than in other districts.

  • These findings highlight the importance of considering both raw counts and spatial context (e.g. population or land area) when comparing crime patterns across districts. Future analysis might benefit from normalizing incidents by district size or population.

  • For this reason, we are choosing to combine and compare datasets using geographic coordinates (longitude and latitude) rather than relying on district-level groupings. This allows for more precise, location-based merging and analysis. We are also planning to seperate crimes based on violent vs property crimes.

5. Merge¶

Now that the data is cleaned, we want a way to merge the STR data and the crime data. We are trying to see the relationship between geographical-specific crime and STR density, so we will link our data by location. We define a "bin" by rounding latitude and longitude to 2 decimal places (about 1 sq. km) and aggregating crime values that fall in the bucket. We also want split violent crime and property crime to see if theres a distinction.

5.1 Using 2-Decimal Precision (about 1km bins)¶

for name, df in [

    ('rental_data_commercial_active', rental_data_commercial_active)

    df['lon_round_p2'] = df['Longitude'].round(2)
    df['coord_key_p2'] = df['lat_round_p2'].astype(str) + "_" + df['lon_round_p2'].astype(str)
    globals()[name] = df

print("coord_key_p2 added to all datasets.")

# Step 3a: Aggregate by TypeText (for individual crime types)
crime_by_type_p2 = (
    crime_data
    .groupby(['coord_key_p2', 'TypeText'])
    .size()
    .unstack(fill_value=0)
    .reset_index()
)

crime_by_type_p2['total_crimes'] = crime_by_type_p2.drop(columns='coord_key_p2').sum(axis=1)
print("Created crime_by_type_p2 with shape:", crime_by_type_p2.shape)

# Step 3b: Aggregate by CrimeCategory for modeling
crime_by_cat_p2 = (
    crime_data
    .groupby(['coord_key_p2', 'CrimeCategory'])
    .size()
    .unstack(fill_value=0)
    .reset_index()
)

crime_by_cat_p2 = crime_by_cat_p2.rename(columns={
    'Violent': 'violent_crime_count',
    'Property': 'property_crime_count'
})
crime_by_cat_p2['crime_count'] = (
    crime_by_cat_p2.get('violent_crime_count', 0) +
    crime_by_cat_p2.get('property_crime_count', 0)
)

# Step 4: Merge both crime summaries into STR datasets
for name in ['rental_data_active', 'rental_data_noncommercial_active', 'rental_data_commercial_active']:
    df = globals()[name]

    # Merge by TypeText
    df = df.merge(crime_by_type_p2, on='coord_key_p2', how='left')

    # Merge by Category
    df = df.merge(crime_by_cat_p2, on='coord_key_p2', how='left')

    # Fill NaNs
    for col in ['violent_crime_count', 'property_crime_count', 'crime_count']:
        if col not in df.columns:
            df[col] = 0
        else:
            df[col] = df[col].fillna(0)

    globals()[name] = df
    print(f"Merged crime data (2-dec precision) onto {name}. New shape: {df.shape}")

    .groupby(['coord_key_p3', 'TypeText'])
    .size()
    .unstack(fill_value=0)
    .reset_index()
)

# Add total crime counts
crime_by_type_p3['total_crimes'] = crime_by_type_p3.drop(columns='coord_key_p3').sum(axis=1)
print(f"crime_by_type_p3 created. Shape: {crime_by_type_p3.shape}")

# Step 4: Merge aggregated crime data onto STR datasets
for name in ['rental_data_active', 'rental_data_noncommercial_active', 'rental_data_commercial_active']:
    df = globals()[name]
    merged_df = df.merge(crime_by_type_p3, on='coord_key_p3', how='left')
    globals()[name] = merged_df
    print(f"Merged crime data (3-dec precision) onto {name}. New shape: {merged_df.shape}")

6.1 Folium map: STRs on the Same Block¶

The most current law in 2023 allows the city to grant only Non-Commercial STR permits (red below) in an attempt to reduce commercial operators from buying property and converting it into short term rentals. Ultimately the move aims to reduce competition for housing for New Orleanians. While Commercial STR ( green below) permits are set to expire later this year, currently there are active commercial permits.

The law also states that only one STR can operate per block. In New Orleans, every block resets the address numbers at the next multiple of 100 (regardless of how many addresses are actualy used). Therefore, if any current, issued permit addresses have the same hundreds digit (but different addresses), we can assume that the properties are on the same block. Let's do a quick check to see if we can find any permits that were issued on the same block:

In [ ]:
from folium.plugins import MarkerCluster

# Step 1: Function to extract block ID from address
def extract_block(address):
    parts = str(address).split()
    if parts and parts[0].isdigit():
        block_number = int(parts[0])
        block = (block_number // 100) * 100  # e.g., 1234 → 1200

    return None

8. Hypothesis¶

We hypothesize that higher densities of Short-Term Rentals (STRs), particularly commercial STRs, are positively correlated with higher property crime rates in New Orleans.

This hypothesis is based on:

  • Exploratory visualizations showing clustering of both STRs and crimes in similar geographic regions
  • Bar and density plots suggesting spatial overlap between commercial STR zones and crime hotspots
  • Correlation analysis indicating a strong positive relationship between STR density and property crime count

To test this hypothesis, we developed a classification model using STR density and STR type (commercial vs non-commercial) to predict whether a given location experiences high property crime.

Our expectation was that these simple spatial features would be predictive of high-crime areas — and our results support that claim.

9. Modeling¶

10. Conclusion¶

In this project, we explored the relationship between Short-Term Rental (STR) density and crime levels across New Orleans. By merging STR permit data with police incident reports, we engineered spatial features (via coordinate rounding) to allow meaningful comparisons at both neighborhood and street-block levels.

Through our exploratory analysis and mapping, we identified that STR density—especially commercial STRs—tends to be more concentrated in areas with higher crime levels, particularly property crime. Violent crime, however, showed little to no correlation with STR presence.

We normalized features and trained a K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) classifier to predict whether a given location would be classified as "high-crime." With only two features (str_count_scaled and is_commercial), our final model achieved:

  • 97% overall accuracy
  • 99% precision and 87% recall for high-crime predictions
  • Strong performance despite modest class imbalance (~21% high-crime zones)

10.1 Key Insights¶

  • Commercial STRs are significantly more correlated with property crime than non-commercial STRs
  • STR density alone is a strong predictor of property crime prevalence
  • Spatial precision (via coord_key) enabled meaningful aggregation for both visual and statistical analysis

10.2 Limitations and Future Work¶

  • Our model does not include time-based features or account for seasonality in crime
  • Incorporating neighborhood demographics or zoning data could enhance predictive power
  • Future iterations might include ensemble methods or try spatial clustering (e.g., DBSCAN) for unsupervised insights

10.3 Takeaway¶

Data science can illuminate spatial-social patterns that aren't always obvious. Our work shows that STR regulation may need to consider not just location, but density thresholds, especially in high-crime, high-STR corridors of the city.
